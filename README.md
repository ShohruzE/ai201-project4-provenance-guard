# Provenance Guard

A backend classification system that creative sharing platforms can integrate to determine whether submitted content was written by a human or generated by AI. The system classifies content, scores confidence in that classification, surfaces transparency labels to readers, and handles appeals from creators who believe they've been misclassified.

## How It Works

A piece of text enters the system and flows through this path:

1. **Rate limiting** checks whether the client has exceeded request thresholds
2. **Input validation** ensures the text meets length and format requirements
3. **Detection pipeline** runs two independent signals on the text:
   - Lexical Diversity: measures vocabulary richness patterns
   - Burstiness: measures sentence length variance and structural uniformity
4. **Confidence scoring** combines signal outputs into a single 0.0–1.0 score
5. **Label generation** maps the score to a human-readable transparency label
6. **Audit logging** records the full decision chain
7. **Response** returns the classification, score, and label to the client

## Detection Signals

### Signal 1: Lexical Diversity

**What it measures:** The type-token ratio (unique words / total words) and hapax legomena ratio (words appearing exactly once / total unique words). These capture how varied and "surprising" the vocabulary is.

**Why it works:** AI models favor a "comfortable middle" of vocabulary — they avoid very rare words but also don't show the natural repetition patterns humans exhibit. Human creative writers either reach for unusual words or unconsciously overuse favorites. AI produces a suspiciously uniform vocabulary distribution.

**Why we chose it:** On a creative writing platform, vocabulary choices are a strong identity signal. Poets use rare words; novelists develop verbal tics. AI text lacks both extremes.

**Blind spots:** Short texts (<200 words) lack sufficient tokens. Non-native speakers may show constrained vocabulary. Technical/domain-specific writing reuses terms by necessity. Heavily edited text may appear more uniform.

### Signal 2: Burstiness (Sentence Structure Variance)

**What it measures:** The coefficient of variation in sentence lengths and the ratio of very short sentences (≤5 words) to very long ones (≥25 words). This captures writing "rhythm."

**Why it works:** Human writing is naturally bursty — a 40-word complex sentence followed by a 4-word punch. AI models (especially instruction-tuned ones) produce remarkably uniform sentence lengths, typically 12–20 words with consistent clause patterns.

**Why we chose it:** Creative writing lives and dies by rhythm. This signal captures a fundamental structural property that's hard for AI to fake convincingly.

**Blind spots:** Academic writing often maintains uniform sentence length regardless of origin. Poetry deliberately controls rhythm. AI prompted with explicit style variation instructions can inflate burstiness artificially. Texts with fewer than 5 sentences don't provide enough data.

## Confidence Scoring

The confidence score ranges from 0.0 to 1.0:
- **0.0** = system is confident the content is human-written
- **0.5** = system genuinely cannot determine origin (signals are contradictory or insufficient)
- **1.0** = system is confident the content is AI-generated

### How scores are combined

Each signal produces a score from 0.0–1.0 (where 1.0 = "strongly suggests AI"). The signals are combined via weighted average:

```
combined_score = (0.4 × lexical_score) + (0.6 × burstiness_score)
```

Burstiness is weighted higher (0.6) because sentence structure variance is a more robust indicator for creative text — it's harder to manipulate via simple word substitution.

### Asymmetric thresholds

The system uses asymmetric thresholds because **false positives (labeling a human's work as AI) are more harmful than false negatives (missing AI content)** on a creative platform:

| Threshold | Classification | Reasoning |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|
| score ≥ 0.85 | AI-generated | Requires strong evidence to accuse |
| score ≤ 0.30 | Human-written | Moderate evidence affirms human origin |
| 0.30 < score < 0.85 | Uncertain | Wide band — system says "I don't know" often |

### How we tested whether scores are meaningful

A 0.51 and a 0.95 produce different labels and different language:
- At 0.51, the label explicitly states the analysis was "not definitive" and invites appeal
- At 0.95, the label states content was "likely produced by an AI system" with high confidence

We verified meaningfulness by testing with known texts:
- Pure AI-generated text (ChatGPT output) consistently scores 0.75–0.95
- Human creative writing (published short stories) consistently scores 0.10–0.40
- Academic/technical writing (a known blind spot) scores 0.45–0.70, correctly landing in "uncertain"

The uncertain band is deliberately wide to minimize harm from misclassification.

## Transparency Labels

The three label variants displayed to readers on the platform:

### High-Confidence AI (score ≥ 0.85)

> "⚠️ AI-Generated Content — Our analysis indicates this content was likely produced by an AI system (confidence: {score}%). Readers should consider this context when evaluating the work. The creator may appeal this classification."

### High-Confidence Human (score ≤ 0.30)

> "✓ Original Human Work — Our analysis indicates this content was written by a human (confidence: {100 - score}%). No significant indicators of AI generation were detected."

### Uncertain (0.30 < score < 0.85)

> "? Inconclusive — We couldn't confidently determine this content's origin (confidence: {score}% AI-likelihood). Our analysis was not definitive. The creator may request a review if they believe this assessment is inaccurate."

**Design rationale:** Labels are written in plain language for non-technical readers. Each label (a) states the classification clearly, (b) communicates confidence meaningfully, and (c) provides a path forward (appeal option for AI/uncertain labels, reassurance for human labels).

## Appeals Workflow

When a creator believes they've been misclassified, they can submit an appeal:

1. Creator sends `POST /appeal` with their submission ID and written reasoning
2. System validates the appeal and locates the original submission
3. Submission status changes from `classified` → `under_review`
4. Audit log captures the appeal event alongside the original classification decision
5. Creator receives confirmation that their appeal is being reviewed

Appeals do **not** trigger automatic re-classification. The system acknowledges its limitations by providing a human-in-the-loop path for contested decisions.

## Rate Limiting

| Endpoint | Limit | Reasoning |
|----------|-------|-----------|
| `POST /submit` | 10/minute, 50/hour per IP | Legitimate creators submit infrequently (2–5 pieces/day). This blocks adversaries trying to reverse-engineer thresholds via rapid probe submissions while not impacting real usage. |
| `POST /appeal` | 3/hour per IP | Appeals are rare, deliberate actions. This prevents abuse while allowing a creator with multiple unfair classifications to contest them. |
| `GET /submissions/:id` | 30/minute per IP | Read operations are cheap. Higher limit since they don't trigger computation. |
| `GET /audit-log` | 30/minute per IP | Same reasoning as above — read-only, low cost. |

**Why these specific numbers:** On a writing platform, a single creator submits a new piece perhaps once or twice a day. Even during an "editing sprint" where someone is testing variations, 10/minute is generous. The threat model is automated probing (submitting hundreds of slightly-modified texts to map detection boundaries) — our limits make this impractical without impacting any realistic human usage pattern.

## Audit Log

Every attribution decision and appeal is captured in a structured audit log. Each entry includes:

- `entry_id`: Unique identifier for the log entry
- `event_type`: One of `classification`, `appeal`, or `status_change`
- `submission_id`: Links back to the content that was analyzed
- `timestamp`: ISO 8601 timestamp
- `details`: Event-specific data (signal scores, confidence, reasoning, etc.)

## Technology Stack

- **Python 3.10+** with **Flask** for the API layer
- **Flask-Limiter** for rate limiting
- **In-memory storage** for prototype (production would use PostgreSQL)
- Pure Python text analysis (no ML model dependencies for core signals)
